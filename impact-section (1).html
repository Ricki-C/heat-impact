An exposure-day is still just a number

We turned warming into very hot days, and very hot days into exposure-days. Now follow the heat into daily life — from who is exposed, to how it harms the body, to where it shows up.

01 · Population exposure

Hot days × people = exposure-days

A very hot summer day is any day above 35°C. Multiply projected very hot days by projected state population and you get person-days of exposure. Sustained increases drive excess mortality, lower productivity, and overloaded health services — hitting the elderly, outdoor workers, and low-income families hardest.

02 · Older adults

Age 65+ are most at risk

Aging weakens the body's ability to cool itself through sweating and circulation, often compounded by chronic conditions and medications. Older adults experience heat-related illness and death at higher rates than any other group, mainly from cardiovascular complications.

03 · Vulnerable populations

Some people can't just stay inside

AC and staying indoors are common solutions, but some groups can't escape exposure. Outdoor workers in construction, agriculture, and delivery endure long hours while generating extra body heat. Low-income communities are more exposed too, with limited AC access and poorer housing.

04 · Humidity vs. hot-dry heat

Not all heat harms the same way

The body cools itself mainly through sweat evaporation. High humidity prevents evaporation, trapping heat and making even moderate temperatures dangerous. Hot-dry conditions allow cooling but cause rapid dehydration. Humid heat is especially hazardous because it disables the body's primary cooling mechanism.

05 · Sleep

Hot nights cut sleep by ~14 minutes

On very warm nights above 30°C, sleep declines by about 14 minutes. More frequent hot nights make people fall asleep later and wake up earlier, compressing the sleep period and reducing sleep quality.

06 · Learning

A hotter school year lowers learning by ~1%

Without air conditioning, a 0.56°C hotter school year reduces that year's learning by about 1%. Hot school days also disproportionately affect minority students, accounting for roughly 5% of the racial achievement gap.

07 · Cooling cost

Summer electricity use rises ~3%

EIA's 2024 forecast expected a 5% rise in cooling degree days to increase average U.S. household summer electricity use by about 3% — a recurring cost that lands hardest on families who can least afford it.

08 · Emergency room visits

Heat pushes up ER visits across categories

Hotter temperatures can increase emergency department visits for injuries, mental health issues, poisonings, and other heat-sensitive conditions, adding pressure to a healthcare system already stretched thin.
